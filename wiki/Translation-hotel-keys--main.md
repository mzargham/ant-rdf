# Translation: The hotel-keys translation chain

Latour's classical demonstration that technologies act — the heavy fob enrols the guest's body where the polite sign could not. Reads through all four Callon moments.

## Moments (in canonical order)

### Problematization

**Problematization: guests leave with keys**

<!-- https://w3id.org/ant/cases/hotel-keys/moment/problematization -->

The manager problematizes: 'guests are leaving with the keys, and we need them back to turn over rooms'.

### Interessement

**Interessement: try a sign, then try a fob**

<!-- https://w3id.org/ant/cases/hotel-keys/moment/interessement -->

The manager tries to interest guests with a polite sign. Sign fails (Latour 1991's key empirical finding). Manager swaps in a heavy fob, cutting off the alternative of pocketing the key comfortably.

### Enrolment

**Enrolment: guests start returning keys**

<!-- https://w3id.org/ant/cases/hotel-keys/moment/enrolment -->

Guests, finding the weighted key uncomfortable in pocket or bag, increasingly return it to the desk on the way out. The fob has enrolled the guest's body into the manager's program.

### Mobilization

**Mobilization: the fob speaks for the manager**

<!-- https://w3id.org/ant/cases/hotel-keys/moment/mobilization -->

Even when the manager is asleep, the fob continues to enforce the program. Latour's point: the network's voice is delegated to a non-human spokesperson that never gets tired.
